# The tool stack

You can deliver your entire first project with free tools. Total monthly cost to start: £0. This is not a compromise position; plenty of working designers deliver paid e-design with exactly this stack for their first year. The only money that needs to move in month one is the client's deposit moving towards you.

The rule for this month: no new tool unless the roadmap asks for it. Tool-shopping feels like progress and costs a week. Everything the 30 days requires is covered by the eight tools below, and you already know how to use half of them.

## The stack at a glance

| Tool | What it does for you | Cost to start | Roadmap moment |
|---|---|---|---|
| Canva | Moodboards, concept boards, proposals, portfolio PDF, one-page site | Free | Weeks 1, 2 and 4 |
| SketchUp Free or Planner 5D | Room layouts and floor plans | Free | Weeks 2 and 4 |
| Pinterest | Client taste discovery, your own reference boards | Free | Weeks 2 and 4 |
| Milanote | Shareable client boards and sourcing lists | Free tier | Week 4 (optional) |
| A measuring app (Apple Measure / Google's measuring tools / magicplan) | Fast room measurements, client self-measurement | Free | Weeks 2 and 4 |
| Stripe payment links or PayPal invoicing | Getting paid by card or transfer, professionally | Free to set up, small fee per transaction | Week 4 |
| Google Forms | The 12-question discovery questionnaire | Free | Weeks 3 and 4 |
| WhatsApp + email you already have | All client communication | Free | Weeks 3 and 4 |

## Canva (the workhorse)

If you learn one tool properly this month, make it Canva. It produces every visual artefact in the kit: the week 2 portfolio pieces, the concept boards you deliver to clients, the one-page proposal from the templates file, and, if you want one, a simple one-page website.

For concept boards, work on an A4 or A3 landscape canvas, drop in product images (screenshot or download from retailer pages for a client deliverable, which is standard practice for private client boards), add a paint palette strip with the exact colour names, and keep to one board per direction. Canva's free plan does everything the roadmap needs; the paid tier's background remover is a nice-to-have for cutting out furniture images, and worth considering only after the first client has paid, from revenue rather than hope.

Also useful: Canva's free PDF export is how your e-design pack becomes one tidy deliverable file, and its brand kit (even on free, set your two or three colours and fonts once) keeps everything you send looking consistent, which reads as established.

## SketchUp Free or Planner 5D (the layout)

You need a credible way to show "sofa here, not there". Two free options; pick whichever feels less annoying within an hour of trying both, and do not learn both.

- **Planner 5D** is the gentler start: drag-and-drop rooms and furniture in 2D and 3D, in the browser or on your phone. Fastest route to a client-friendly layout image.
- **SketchUp Free** (the browser version) has a steeper first evening but more precision, and it is the one you can grow into if you fall in love with layouts.

A hand-drawn plan on grid paper, photographed in good light and labelled neatly, is also completely acceptable for a room refresh brief. The client is buying the decision, not the software. Do not let a 3D render habit eat your fixed fee's hours.

## Pinterest (the taste extractor)

Two uses. First, question 7 of the discovery questionnaire asks clients for three rooms they love, and the easiest way is a small shared Pinterest board: ask them to pin 10 to 15 images, then read the board like an x-ray (they will say "minimalist" and pin fifteen warm, cluttered, bookish rooms; believe the pins, not the adjectives). Second, keep your own reference boards per project, set to private, so clients see conclusions rather than your working.

## Milanote (the client board, optional)

Milanote is a moodboard-meets-pinboard tool that many e-designers use for the client-facing pack: images, notes, links and the shopping list on one shareable board the client can comment on. The free tier (around 100 elements) covers roughly one active project at a time, which is exactly what you have. Entirely optional: a Canva PDF plus a link list does the same job. Use Milanote if your client is chatty and collaborative, a PDF if they just want the answer.

## A measuring app (the site visit accelerator)

For your own visits, your phone probably already measures: Measure on iPhone, or Google's equivalent on Android, is accurate enough for furniture-level decisions; magicplan's free tier will sketch a floor plan as you walk the room. For e-design clients measuring their own rooms, do not rely on apps at all: the questionnaire's instruction (each wall's length, ceiling height, windows, doors, radiators, with any tape measure) fails less often than a nervous client waving a phone at a wall. Always sanity-check client measurements against their photos before you design; a "4 metre" wall holding a 3-seat sofa and two alcoves is telling you something.

## Stripe payment links or PayPal invoicing (the getting-paid part)

A plain bank transfer is completely fine for client one, and it is fee-free; the invoice template in file 04 includes the details block. Add a card option because it removes friction at the exact moment you want none:

- **Stripe payment links:** no monthly cost; you create a link ("Deposit, e-design package, £125"), send it, they pay by card, done. Stripe takes a small percentage per transaction (currently a low single-digit percentage plus pence in the UK; check their pricing page for today's rate).
- **PayPal invoicing:** similar per-transaction fee, and some clients trust the familiar name, which is worth something with deposits from strangers.

Pick one, set it up in week 4 when the first proposal goes out, not before. Whichever you use, the 50% deposit rule from the agreement stands: link sent with the agreement, work starts when it is paid.

## Google Forms (the questionnaire)

Put the 12 questions from the templates file into a Google Form once, in week 2 or 3, and every future client gets a tidy link instead of a wall of email text, while their answers land in a spreadsheet you keep forever. Turn on "collect email addresses", add a file-upload question for their room photos if you are signed into a Google account, and paste the form's link into your enquiry reply template. Ten minutes of setup, permanently useful.

## What you do not need this month

To save you the browsing: no website builder subscription (the Canva one-pager or an Instagram bio covers it), no CAD software, no rendering suites, no CRM (a phone note tracking conversations is your CRM until roughly client five), no accounting software (a spreadsheet of money in and money out is fine for now), no paid stock photos, no scheduling tool (you are booking one call, not a calendar), and no courses on any of the above. Every one of these becomes worth revisiting at some point, and that point is after paying clients exist.

Total to start: £0 per month, plus a transaction fee of a few pounds when, and only when, someone pays you. That is the correct direction for money to flow in your first month.
