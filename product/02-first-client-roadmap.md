# The first client roadmap

Thirty days, four weeks, one paying client. Each week has one job. Do the week's job, tick the "done when" list at the end, move on. Do not read ahead and start doing week 3 before week 1 is finished; the order is the method.

A note before you start: everything here assumes the smallest viable version. One lane, one offer, one price, three portfolio pieces, three circles of outreach, one client. Small is not a compromise. Small is what makes it finishable in a month while you have a job, children or both.

---

## Week 1: pick a lane and define one offer

The single biggest reason aspiring designers never get a first client is that they offer "interior design" in general, which forces every potential client to work out what that means, what it costs and whether it is for them. Nobody does that work. A specific offer with a fixed price does it for them.

### Day 1 to 2: pick one lane

There are four beginner lanes that reliably produce £75 to £500 briefs. Read all four, pick one. You are not marrying it; you are picking a door to walk through first.

**1. E-design.** The client sends you photos, measurements and a budget. You send back a concept board, a floor plan or layout suggestion, and a clickable shopping list. Everything happens over email; you never visit. Best if: you want to work evenings around a job or children, you are comfortable with Canva and a layout tool, and you would rather write than talk. Typical first fee: £150 to £400 for one room.

**2. Room refresh (in person).** One room, one day, mostly using what the client already owns plus a small shopping list. You rearrange, restyle, rehang, and leave the room photographably better by 5pm. Best if: you are hands-on, local, and your friends already ask you to "come and look at" their rooms. Typical first fee: £200 to £450 for the day.

**3. Styling for sellers and landlords.** You walk a property before it is listed and produce a room-by-room action plan (declutter this, paint that, move this, buy these three things) so it photographs and views better. Estate agents and landlords are repeat buyers of this. Best if: you like before-and-after logic more than self-expression, and you want a route to repeat work fast. Typical first fee: £100 to £250 per property for the walk-through and report.

**4. Colour consultation.** Ninety minutes in the client's home (or on a video call with their photos), then a written colour plan: exact paint colours per wall, finishes, and how the rooms flow together. Best if: colour is your genuine strong suit and you want the lowest-priced, fastest-to-deliver starter offer. Typical first fee: £75 to £150.

Pick using two questions. Which of these have people already, unprompted, asked you for? And which could you deliver next week without learning a new skill? Where the answers overlap, that is your lane.

### Day 3 to 5: define your starter offer

An offer is one sentence a stranger can understand plus a fixed price. Fill this in:

> **I help [who] [get what outcome] with [deliverable], for a fixed fee of £[X], delivered within [timeframe].**

Examples of the finished sentence:

- "I help busy renters make their living room feel put together with a one-room e-design pack (concept board, layout and shopping list), for a fixed fee of £250, delivered within 10 days."
- "I help home sellers get better viewings with a pre-listing styling walk-through and room-by-room action plan, for a fixed fee of £150, delivered within 48 hours of the visit."

Now write down exactly what is included, because scope you did not write down is scope you will give away free. For a one-room e-design, a sensible v1 is: one concept board, one layout suggestion, one shopping list of up to 15 items with links, one round of revisions, delivered as a PDF within 10 days. That is it. No second room "while you're at it", no project managing the purchases.

Set the price using the pricing guide (file 03) and the pricing calculator spreadsheet. Do it this week, not vaguely later; an offer without a number is a hobby.

### Day 6 to 7: write it down where people can see it

You do not need a website this month. You need one place to point people to. Choose one:

- An Instagram account with your offer in the bio and, by end of week 2, your three portfolio pieces as posts, or
- A one-page Canva site or PDF (Canva makes both free) with the offer sentence, what is included, the price, three portfolio images and how to contact you.

Write the offer sentence, what is included, the price and one line about you (two sentences, warm, no fake credentials, no invented years of experience: "I'm [name], I design calm, liveable rooms for real budgets. This is what I'd do with yours.").

### Week 1 is done when

- [ ] You have chosen one lane and can say why in one sentence.
- [ ] Your offer sentence is written, with a deliverable, a fixed price and a timeframe.
- [ ] The included/not-included list exists in writing.
- [ ] You have one public place (Instagram bio or one-pager) stating the offer and price.
- [ ] You have said the price out loud to another human without adding "but I could do it cheaper".

## Week 2: build a three-piece portfolio without clients

Nobody hires a designer with no pictures. Fortunately, "portfolio" does not mean "past clients". It means "evidence of your eye and your process". You can create three legitimate pieces this week.

### Piece 1: your own room (days 8 to 10)

Redesign one room in your own home as if a client had paid your fee. Follow your own offer exactly: if you sell e-design, produce the real concept board, layout and shopping list for your room; if you sell a refresh day, actually spend a day refreshing the room. Then document it properly:

- Photograph before and after in daylight, phone camera is fine, landscape orientation, curtains open, lights warm, lens wiped.
- Write 100 to 150 words on the brief you set yourself, the constraints (budget, rental rules, toddler) and the three decisions that made the difference.

Constraints are the interesting part. "Made a rented flat feel warmer without painting" is a better story than a big budget makeover, and it is the story your first clients live in.

### Piece 2: a friend's room, free, in exchange for photos and a review (days 8 to 13, runs alongside)

Do one room for a friend or relative at no charge, on explicit terms. Send this message today, because their timeline is the bottleneck:

> Hi [name], I'm setting up as an interior designer and I need one practice project before I start charging. Can I do your [living room/bedroom]? Completely free. You'd get [exactly what your paid offer includes]. In return I'd want three things: you actually let me finish it, I can photograph before and after for my portfolio, and if you're happy you write me two honest sentences I can quote. Interested?

Three notes. Pick the friend whose taste overlaps with the clients you want, not the closest friend. Deliver it exactly as if they had paid, including your questionnaire and timeframe, because this is a rehearsal of your whole process, not a favour. And get the two-sentence review in writing the day you finish, while they are delighted; the templates file has the exact request message.

### Piece 3: a fictional concept board, clearly labelled (days 13 to 14)

Invent a realistic brief and design it. For example: "Concept: a north-facing Victorian terrace living room for a couple with a £600 budget who cannot paint." Produce your full deliverable for it and label it visibly: **"Concept project (self-initiated brief)"**.

Never pass a concept off as client work. Not because you would get caught, though you might, but because your whole standing with early clients rests on being straight with them, and honest labelling costs you nothing. Agents and clients see concept boards in every designer's portfolio; they read as skill, not as padding.

### Present all three (day 14)

Put the three pieces in one place: three Instagram posts (before/after images plus the 100-word story in the caption) or three pages in a Canva PDF. Each piece needs images, the brief, the constraints and what you did. That is a portfolio. It is enough. Resist the urge to spend another fortnight polishing it; week 3 is where clients actually come from.

### Week 2 is done when

- [ ] Your own room: photographed before and after, with a written 100-word story.
- [ ] Friend's room: delivered as if paid, photographed, and the two-sentence review is in your possession in writing.
- [ ] One fictional concept, produced in full and labelled "Concept project (self-initiated brief)".
- [ ] All three live in one shareable place, and you have sent the link to one person for a sense check.

## Week 3: outreach for people who hate outreach

You do not need an audience, an advert or the courage to film yourself. You need to inform three concentric circles of warm-ish people that a clear offer exists. This is the "3 warm circles" method. Total sends this week: roughly one announcement, three group posts and ten emails. All scripts below are word for word; change the bracketed parts and nothing else until you have tested them as written.

Two rules first. Rule one: every message names the offer, the price range or price, and asks a question at the end (messages that end with a question get replies; messages that end with information get silence). Rule two: you are not asking anyone to hire you. You are asking them to think of who might. That reframe is what makes this bearable for introverts, and it also genuinely works better.

### Circle 1 (day 15): the people who already know you

Send this to your WhatsApp broadcast list, family group chats, and as an Instagram story or Facebook post. Personal message version:

> Hi [name], small announcement: I've started taking on interior design clients. My first offer is [offer sentence, e.g. "a one-room e-design pack: concept board, layout and shopping list for £250"]. I've got [two/three] spots this month at that starter price while I build my portfolio. I'm not assuming you need this! But you know everyone. Who's the first person that comes to mind who's been moaning about a room in their house?

Public post version:

> Some news: I've started taking on interior design clients. First offer: [offer sentence with price]. Three spots this month at the starter price. If a room in your house has been quietly annoying you for a year, or you know someone whose room has, send me a message and I'll tell you exactly how it works. [Link to portfolio]

Why "who comes to mind?" instead of "do you need this?": it removes the awkwardness of a friend having to reject you, and it turns 30 contacts into 30 scouts. Expect replies within 48 hours; answer every single one, including the ones that are just "good for you!", warmly and briefly.

### Circle 2 (days 16 to 18): local groups

Find three local Facebook groups (your town's community group, a local "home and garden" or renovation group, a local parents' group) and your Nextdoor neighbourhood. Read each group's rules first; many have a specific day for business posts, and posting outside it burns goodwill. Then post:

> Hello neighbours. I'm a local interior designer just starting out, and I'm looking for my first [two/three] clients at a reduced starter price. The offer: [offer sentence, price included]. Here's a recent room I did: [one before/after photo, or link]. I'm local to [area], happy to answer any questions in the comments. If your [living room/bedroom] has been on your "sort it out eventually" list, what's the thing that bothers you most about it?

Notes on this one. The before/after photo is doing most of the work, so lead with your strongest. "Just starting out, starter price" is not weakness, it is the entire reason a bargain-hunting group member acts now instead of someday. And answer comments with actual mini-advice ("that sounds like a layout problem more than a furniture problem, try floating the sofa off that wall"); every helpful comment is a public audition.

### Circle 3 (days 18 to 21): ten local estate agents, letting agents and landlords

This is the circle most beginners skip, and it is the one that produces repeat work. Agents have a constant supply of properties that photograph badly, sell slowly or sit empty between tenants. Find ten: every estate and letting agent branch in your area, plus any landlords you know personally. Email the branch manager by name where you can find it. Send this:

> Subject: Making [branch/agency name]'s listings photograph better
>
> Hi [first name],
>
> I'm [your name], a local interior stylist in [area]. I help sellers and landlords get properties looking their best before photos and viewings: a walk-through, then a room-by-room action plan the owner can do in a weekend, mostly using what's already in the house.
>
> For sellers, that typically means better photos and fewer "we just couldn't picture living there" viewings. For landlords, it means a tired rental presenting well without a refurb budget.
>
> My fixed fee is £[X] per property, and I'm offering the first one for [free / half price] for one local agency so you can judge the results yourself. Here's a recent before and after: [link or attached image].
>
> Would it be useful if I dropped into the branch for ten minutes this week? I can bring a one-page example report.
>
> [Name]
> [Phone number]
> [Portfolio link]

Send ten. Expect most to ignore you; that is normal and not a verdict on you. Then, and this is the part that actually converts, follow up once after four or five days:

> Hi [first name], just floating this back up. If it's easier, I could do a free walk-through on whichever current listing has been on the market longest and send you the report, no strings. Would that be worth a try?

A specific, free, zero-risk first step ("your stalest listing") is very hard for a busy manager to say no to, and one good report tends to turn into a standing arrangement.

### Days 20 to 21: keep the pot warm

Reply to everything within a day. Log every conversation somewhere simple (a note on your phone is fine): name, date, what they said, next step. Anyone who said "maybe" or "after payday" gets one friendly nudge in 7 to 10 days: "No pressure at all, just letting you know I've got one starter-price spot left for [month]. Want me to hold it?" That is scarcity you can say honestly, because you genuinely can only deliver a few briefs a month around your life.

### Week 3 is done when

- [ ] Circle 1 announcement sent personally to at least 20 people and posted publicly once.
- [ ] Circle 2 post made in at least three local groups (within their rules) with your best before/after.
- [ ] Circle 3: ten agent/landlord emails sent, follow-ups scheduled in your calendar.
- [ ] Every reply answered within 24 hours and logged.
- [ ] At least one live conversation is happening. (If genuinely none: see "if week 3 is quiet" below before doing anything drastic.)

## Week 4: discovery call, quote, close, deliver

Someone has said "ooh, tell me more". Here is the path from that message to money in your account and a finished room. The templates file (04) has every document mentioned below written out in full.

### The discovery call (days 22 to 24)

Move interested people to a 20-minute call quickly: "Easiest is a quick call so I can hear about the room. Are you free for 20 minutes on [two options]?" On the call, your only jobs are to listen, to scope, and to say the price without flinching. A structure that works:

1. **Them, 10 minutes.** "Talk me through the room. What happens in it, what do you hate about it, what have you already tried?" Take notes. Ask what they have spent money on before and what the room budget is. If they say they have no budget in mind, offer bands: "Most people I talk to are working with £300 to £800 for a room like this. Where do you sit?"
2. **You, 5 minutes.** Reflect the problem back in their words, then describe your offer as the answer: what they get, when, and the fixed price. Then stop talking. The silence after the price feels enormous; let it sit. The first person to speak after a price is quoted usually adjusts it, and it should not be you.
3. **Next step, 5 minutes.** "I'll send you a one-page proposal today with everything we discussed. If you're happy, you reply yes, I invoice 50% to book you in, and I can start on [date]." No hard closing. A clear next step is the close.

If the call reveals structural hopes (walls, extensions, plumbing), say the ready-made line from file 01 and design around it.

### The quote (same day as the call)

Send the one-page proposal from the templates file the same day, while the conversation is warm. It contains: their situation in two sentences, what you will deliver, timeline, the fixed price, the two things you need from them, and the yes-instruction. Same-day proposals close at a dramatically higher rate than "I'll put something together this week", for the boring reason that enthusiasm decays.

If they go quiet, nudge once after three days ("Any questions on the proposal? Happy to tweak the scope if the fit isn't quite right") and once a week later. Then leave it; two nudges is diligence, three is pressure.

If they say it is expensive, the pricing guide (file 03) has the exact script. Short version: do not discount; shrink the scope instead.

### The close (days 24 to 26)

When they say yes: send the letter of agreement and the 50% deposit invoice from the templates file. **Work starts when the deposit lands, not when the yes lands.** This is the single most protective habit in the kit. Send the discovery questionnaire (also in the templates file) at the same time, so they are usefully busy while you wait for the transfer.

### Deliver (days 26 to 30)

- Confirm receipt of the deposit and questionnaire with a warm one-liner and the delivery date.
- Do the work exactly as scoped. Where you are torn between two directions, choose the one their questionnaire and Pinterest board point to, not your personal taste; the first client is buying their room, improved, not your manifesto.
- Deliver on or before the promised date, with a short friendly note walking them through the deliverable ("Start with page 2, that's the big idea; the shopping list is ordered by impact per pound").
- One revision round, as scoped. Then invoice the balance, and once it is paid, send the testimonial request from the templates file within 48 hours of their happy reply, plus permission to use the photos.

Then do the ten-minute debrief that turns one client into a business: what did they ask for that was not in scope (candidate for a future offer)? What took longer than estimated (feed the real hours back into the pricing calculator)? Who did they mention that might need you? Send one message to that person. That is client two starting.

### Week 4 is done when

- [ ] Discovery call held, notes taken, price said out loud without apology.
- [ ] Proposal sent the same day as the call.
- [ ] Agreement signed and 50% deposit received before any work started.
- [ ] Work delivered on time, one revision round completed, balance invoiced and paid.
- [ ] Testimonial and photo permission requested within 48 hours of delivery.
- [ ] Real hours logged in the pricing calculator, and one referral message sent.

## If week 3 is quiet

Sometimes the month runs long. Do not conclude "this doesn't work" or, worse, "I don't work". Diagnose in this order, because these are the failure points in order of likelihood:

1. **Volume.** Did 40+ real humans actually receive a message with the offer and price in it? Count. Most "nobody replied" cases are actually "I posted once and told four people". Finish the sends first.
2. **Clarity.** Show your offer sentence to two honest people and ask them to repeat back what you sell and what it costs. If they cannot, rewrite the sentence, not the price.
3. **Proof.** Is your best before/after genuinely good, and leading every message? If your strongest image is weak, spend two evenings restyling and reshooting your own room. Photography is cheap to redo.
4. **Price.** This is the last suspect, not the first, and the fix is rarely "lower". See the pricing guide before touching the number.

Then run week 3 again with the weak link fixed: a second round in new groups, ten more agent emails, one more announcement ("last starter-price spot for [next month]"). Nearly everyone who follows the roadmap to a first client sends more messages than feels comfortable and fewer than they feared. The offer is built, the proof exists, the scripts are in your hands. Keep sending.
