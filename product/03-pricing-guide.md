# The pricing guide

Pricing is where most first clients are lost, and almost never because the number was too high. They are lost because the beginner offers "my rate is £25 an hour, it depends how long it takes", which hands the client a maths problem, an open-ended risk and a reason to say "let me think about it". This guide gives you the alternative: one fixed price per offer, set deliberately, said calmly.

Everything here refers to the companion spreadsheet, `pricing-calculator.csv`. Open it in Excel or Google Sheets now and keep it open while you read; it does the arithmetic in this guide automatically.

## Fixed fees, not hourly. Here is why.

Charge a fixed fee for a defined deliverable. Not an hourly rate. Four reasons:

1. **Clients can say yes to a number.** "£250 for a full e-design of your living room" is a decision. "£25 an hour" is a negotiation followed by anxiety about every email they send you.
2. **Hourly punishes you for getting better.** The faster and sharper you get, the less an hourly designer earns for the same result. Fixed fees mean your improving skill raises your effective rate instead of cutting your invoice.
3. **Hourly invites scope arguments.** Nobody disputes a fixed fee for a delivered thing. People absolutely dispute whether something "really took" nine hours.
4. **You do not yet know how long things take.** That is fine, and it is exactly what the calculator is for. You estimate hours privately, price on the estimate, then log real hours and let the truth correct your next quote. The client never sees any of this.

The one habit that makes fixed fees safe is the one you set in week 1: write down what is included, including how many revision rounds, and treat everything else as a new quote.

## How to set the number

The formula is deliberately unglamorous:

> (Estimated hours, all of them) x (target hourly rate) + (materials and expenses) = suggested price. Then round to a clean number.

**Estimated hours means all of them.** The design time, yes, but also the discovery call, the questionnaire read-through, the messages, the sourcing rabbit holes, the revision round, travel if you visit. Beginners typically count half their real hours. When in doubt, take your instinct and add 40%.

**Target hourly rate: £20 to £35 is a sensible beginner band in the UK.** Below £20 you are earning less than many jobs that require no evening Canva sessions, and clients start to wonder what is wrong. Above £35 is entirely achievable, just usually not on client one. Pick a number in the band that you can say out loud; you will raise it soon (see below).

**Materials and expenses:** printing, travel, parking, props for a refresh day, any paid tool. Small, but it comes off your earnings if you forget it.

**Round to a clean, confident number.** £237.50 sounds calculated by a nervous spreadsheet. £250 sounds like a fee. Round to the nearest £5 below £100 and the nearest £25 above it.

## Typical UK beginner ranges

These are typical starter ranges for new designers in the UK market. They are research-informed but they are ranges, not rules: adjust for your local market (London and the South East sit at or above the top of each band; small towns and rural markets nearer the bottom, and the same logic applies across European markets, where established e-design pricing is broadly similar).

| Starter offer | Typical beginner range |
|---|---|
| Mini moodboard or colour consultation | £75 to £150 |
| Single-room e-design package (board, layout, shopping list) | £150 to £400 |
| In-person room refresh day | £200 to £450 |
| Styling for sellers walk-through and report | £100 to £250 |

Where to land inside the band: start at roughly the lower third for client one, not the bottom. The bottom of a band attracts the most price-sensitive clients, who are, universally, the most demanding ones. A £250 e-design client is usually easier to work with than a £150 one. If your three portfolio pieces from week 2 are strong, the middle of the band is defensible from day one.

One deliberate exception to "never go low": a single strategic freebie or half-price job with a professional (the estate agent's stalest listing, from the week 3 script) is fine, because you are explicitly buying a relationship and a case study, on your terms, once. That is different from drifting cheap because you were scared on a phone call.

## The psychology of the first price

Some truths to hold onto in week 4:

**The price feels bigger to you than to them.** You are comparing £250 to your hourly job wage. The client is comparing it to a £900 sofa they nearly bought, a £3,000 quote from an established studio, or five more years of hating the room. In their mental accounting, a fixed £250 to finally sort the living room is one of the cheaper items in the whole project.

**Say the number, then stop talking.** The kit's single most valuable sentence of negotiation advice: after "the fee is £250", go silent. Nervous beginners fill the silence with discounts nobody asked for ("...but I could do £200 since it's my first..."). The client was probably just thinking about which room to start with.

**Never apologise for or justify the price with your inexperience.** You may say "starter price while I build my portfolio" in outreach, where it creates urgency, but in the discovery call the fee is simply the fee. "It's £250, that includes the concept board, the layout, the shopping list and one round of revisions." Full stop.

**A price that makes you slightly wince is usually right.** If you feel completely comfortable, you are almost certainly under; if you feel sick, you have overshot your current nerve. Slight wince is the calibration point.

## When someone says "that's expensive"

First, know that this sentence usually means one of three things, and only one of them is about money: "I don't yet see what I get for it" (most common), "it's more than the random number in my head", or genuinely "I cannot afford it". Your response handles all three without discounting. Word for word:

> "That's fair, it's a real investment. Can I ask what you were expecting to spend? ...
>
> [If their number is close:] Okay, we're not far apart. The £250 covers [restate the deliverables and the outcome in their words: "so you're not spending another £600 on furniture that doesn't fix the layout"]. I'd rather keep everything in than cut corners, but if the budget is £180, I can do a slimmed version: the concept board and shopping list, without the layout drawing and revision round. Would that work better?
>
> [If their number is miles away, or it's genuinely unaffordable:] I completely understand, and I'd rather you wait than stretch. If it's useful, my colour consultation is £95 and it solves the biggest piece of most rooms. And I'm happy to stay in touch for when the timing's right."

The rule underneath the script: **shrink the scope, never the price of the scope.** A discount teaches the client (and you) that your prices are opening bids. A smaller package at an honest price keeps your rate intact, gives them a real choice, and very often, once they compare the slim version with the full one, they buy the full one anyway.

And sometimes "no" is the right outcome. A client who grinds you down before paying is a preview of the entire project. Losing them is not a failure of the roadmap; it is the roadmap working as a filter.

## When to raise your price

Raise sooner than feels natural, on a simple trigger system:

- **After client one delivers and the testimonial is in:** raise 10 to 20%. The starter price existed to compensate for having no proof. You now have proof.
- **Every time two clients in a row say yes without hesitation:** raise again. Instant yeses mean the market values this above your number.
- **When you log real hours in the calculator and your effective rate has slipped** (the job took 14 hours, not 10): re-price the offer using the real hours rather than accepting the pay cut.
- **When you are busier than you want to be:** price is your queue length control. Too many enquiries at £250 is the market telling you it is a £300 offer.

Raising prices never requires an announcement, an apology or a justification to anyone. New price, next enquiry, said calmly. Existing quotes are honoured; that is all.

## Housekeeping in one paragraph

Take 50% upfront before starting, always (the agreement and invoice in the templates file are built for this). Keep a simple record of income and expenses from the first pound; in the UK, register for Self Assessment with HMRC once you have self-employed income, and note that a small trading allowance exists for tiny turnover, but registering is cheap insurance either way. Elsewhere in Europe, your national equivalent applies. Prices in this kit assume you are nowhere near VAT registration territory in year one, which for a first client is safely true. None of this paragraph is tax advice; a one-off conversation with a local accountant when money starts arriving is worth its fee.
