# Client templates

Six documents cover the entire journey from "someone messaged me" to "someone recommended me". They are all here, written in full. Copy them into your own email or Canva doc, replace everything in [brackets], delete anything that does not apply, and send. The example texts assume a £250 single-room e-design; adjust the deliverables and numbers to your own offer.

A once-only setup tip: save each of these as a draft or note on your phone now, in week 1, so that when a real enquiry arrives you respond in minutes, not evenings. Speed reads as professionalism.

---

## Template A: the enquiry reply email

Send this within a few hours of any enquiry, even a vague one. Its jobs are to be warm, to show there is a process, and to get them onto a call.

> Subject: Your [living room] (lovely to hear from you)
>
> Hi [name],
>
> Thanks so much for getting in touch, and yes, this sounds exactly like the kind of project I take on.
>
> A little about how I work: my [one-room e-design package] is a fixed £[250], and it includes [a concept board, a furniture layout, a shopping list with links, and one round of revisions], delivered within [10 days] of booking. No hourly rates, no surprises.
>
> The best next step is a quick 20-minute call so I can hear about the room, what's driving you mad about it, and what you'd love it to be. From there I'll send you a simple one-page proposal, and you can decide with everything in front of you. No obligation at any point.
>
> I could do [Tuesday evening] or [Thursday lunchtime]. Do either of those work? If a call's tricky, I'm happy to do it over email instead.
>
> Warm regards,
> [Name]
> [Phone] | [Portfolio link]

Notes: offer exactly two time slots (open-ended "when suits you?" stalls people), always state the fixed price in this first reply (it filters kindly, and hiding prices creates awkward calls), and keep the email-instead option, because some of your best clients are as introverted as you are.

## Template B: the client discovery questionnaire (12 questions)

Send after booking (deposit paid), or before a discovery call if the client prefers writing to talking. Put it in a Google Form (see the tool stack file) or simply paste it into an email. Preface it with:

> This takes about 15 minutes and it's genuinely the foundation of the whole design, so pour a cuppa and be as honest and rambly as you like. There are no wrong answers, and "I hate it but I don't know why" is useful information.

1. Who uses this room, and what actually happens in it on a normal Tuesday? (People, pets, work, telly, toys, everything.)
2. What do you dislike most about the room right now? What's the first thing that annoys you when you walk in?
3. Is there anything about the room that already works, or that you love and want to keep? (Furniture, objects, the light at a certain time of day.)
4. Which items are staying no matter what, and which can go? Be ruthless where you can be.
5. What's your total budget for changes to this room, not counting my fee? A range is fine, and honest beats optimistic.
6. Do you own or rent? If renting, what are you allowed to change (paint, hanging things, fixtures)?
7. Show me three rooms you love: Pinterest links, Instagram saves, screenshots, magazine photos, anything. A sentence on what draws you to each would be gold.
8. Now one room you actively dislike, and why. (This is often more revealing than the loves.)
9. Are there colours you're drawn to, and any you can't live with? Any strong feelings about pattern, wood tones, or metals?
10. How should this room feel when it's done, in three words? (Calm, cosy, energising, grown-up, playful...)
11. Any practical constraints I should design around? (Radiators, awkward alcoves, storage you can't lose, a cat that shreds rattan, small children and pale bouclé.)
12. Anything else you want me to know, including anything you've tried before that didn't work?

Also request, in the same message: photos of every wall of the room taken from the opposite corner, a photo out of the window (light matters), and measurements: the length of each wall, ceiling height, and the position and size of windows, doors and radiators. Tell them a phone measuring app or any tape measure is fine, and that centimetres of precision are not required for a refresh, roughly right beats not sent.

## Template C: the one-page proposal

Send the same day as the discovery call. One page, five short sections. Structure, then a full example.

**Structure:** 1) Their situation, in their own words. 2) What I'll deliver. 3) Timeline. 4) The fee. 5) What happens next.

**Example text:**

> **Proposal: [Sarah], living room e-design**
> [Date]
>
> **The brief.** Your living room has to be a playroom by day and a grown-up space by 8pm, and right now it's neither: the layout wastes the bay window, the storage is losing the battle with the toys, and the room has never felt finished since you moved in. You'd like it calm, warm and sorted, working with the existing sofa, on a furnishing budget of around £600.
>
> **What I'll deliver.**
> - A concept board setting the direction: palette, mood, and the key pieces.
> - A furniture layout for the room, drawn to your measurements, including a toy-storage plan that doesn't dominate the room.
> - A shopping list of up to 15 items with links and prices, kept inside your £600, ordered by impact so you can phase purchases if you prefer.
> - One round of revisions after you've lived with the design for a few days.
>
> **Timeline.** Delivered as a PDF within 10 days of booking. The revision round adds up to 5 days after your feedback.
>
> **The fee.** £250 fixed. 50% (£125) to book, the balance on delivery. No other charges.
>
> **Next step.** Reply "yes" to this email and I'll send over a simple letter of agreement and the booking invoice. Your slot is provisionally held for 7 days.
>
> [Name] | [Phone] | [Portfolio link]

Notes: the brief section is the sales engine of the page; if the client thinks "she completely got it", the rest is a formality, so use their exact phrases from the call. Keep it to one page always. The 7-day hold is honest gentle urgency, not a countdown timer.

## Template D: the letter of agreement

**This is a template, not legal advice. Have it checked by a solicitor or legal adviser for your country before relying on it, and adjust it to your offer.** It is written in plain English on purpose: a first client is reassured by clarity and alarmed by ten pages of boilerplate. Send it as a PDF or in the body of an email; a reply of "I agree" plus the paid deposit is workable evidence of agreement at this scale, and a typed name and date at the bottom is even better.

> **Letter of agreement**
>
> Between: [Your name], trading as [business name], of [address] ("I", "me")
> And: [Client name], of [address] ("you")
> Date: [date]
> Project: [One-room e-design of the living room at the above address]
>
> **1. What I will do.** I will provide the following, and only the following: [concept board; furniture layout based on the measurements you supply; shopping list of up to 15 items with links; one round of revisions]. Anything beyond this (additional rooms, extra revision rounds, sourcing visits, project management of purchases) can be quoted separately if you'd like it.
>
> **2. What this is not.** I provide interior design and decoration advice only. I am not an architect, structural engineer or surveyor, and nothing I supply is structural, architectural, electrical, gas or plumbing advice. Any works of that kind need advice from a suitably qualified and, where applicable, registered professional, and any required planning permission or building regulations approval remains your responsibility. My designs assume no structural changes unless a qualified professional you engage has approved them.
>
> **3. Fees and payment.** The fixed fee is £[250]. 50% (£[125]) is payable to book, and work begins once it arrives. The balance is payable [on delivery of the final design / before release of final files], within [7] days of invoice. Where you buy items from the shopping list, you buy them directly: retailers are responsible for their products, prices and stock levels may change after delivery of the design, and I receive no commission unless stated in writing.
>
> **4. Timings.** I will deliver within [10 days] of receiving your deposit, completed questionnaire, photos and measurements. If I'm delayed for reasons on my side, I'll tell you promptly and agree a new date; if the delay is longer than [14 days] you may cancel under clause 6 with a full refund of the deposit.
>
> **5. Revisions.** The fee includes [one] round of revisions, meaning one consolidated list of change requests within the original brief. Further rounds, or changes to the brief itself (a new budget, different retained furniture, a different function for the room), are quoted separately before any work is done.
>
> **6. Cancellation.** You may cancel before work starts for a full refund of the deposit. Once work has started, the deposit covers work done and is non-refundable, but you'll owe nothing further if you cancel before delivery. If you are a consumer and we agreed this contract at a distance (email, phone, online), you may have a legal 14-day cooling-off right under your local consumer law (in the UK, the Consumer Contracts Regulations 2013); by asking me to start work within that period you agree that, if you then cancel, you'll pay for work done up to cancellation.
>
> **7. Your part.** You'll supply honest information, photos and measurements, and respond to reasonable questions within a few days, since the timeline depends on it. Measurements you supply are relied on as accurate.
>
> **8. Using the work.** The design is for your personal use for this property. I keep the right to photograph or reproduce the design and (with your prior consent) photos of the finished room in my portfolio and marketing. Just say if you'd rather stay anonymous, that's completely fine.
>
> **9. Liability.** I'll carry out the work with reasonable care and skill. I'm not liable for the acts of retailers or tradespeople you engage, or for losses beyond the fee paid, except for anything the law does not allow me to exclude. [I hold professional indemnity and public liability insurance with [insurer].]
>
> **10. The legal bits.** This agreement is governed by the law of [England and Wales / Scotland / your country], and it's the whole agreement between us for this project. Neither of us intends anything here to be unfriendly; it exists so we both know exactly where we stand.
>
> Agreed:
> [Your name, date]
> [Client name, date]

## Template E: the invoice

Two invoices per project: deposit and balance. Same layout, different description line. You can build this in Stripe or PayPal (see the tool stack) or send it as a PDF or email. Legally an invoice is mostly just clear information; here is the text.

> **INVOICE**
>
> Invoice number: [2026-001] (any consistent sequence, never repeat one)
> Date: [date] | Due: [on receipt / within 7 days]
>
> From: [Your name, trading as business name, address, email, phone]
> To: [Client name, address]
>
> **Description**
> Deposit (50%) to book: one-room e-design package, living room at [address], as per proposal dated [date] .......... £125.00
>
> [Balance invoice version: Balance (50%) on delivery: one-room e-design package, living room at [address] .......... £125.00]
>
> **Total due: £125.00**
> [If UK and not VAT registered: Not VAT registered; no VAT has been charged.]
>
> **How to pay**
> Bank transfer to:
> Account name: [name]
> Bank: [bank]
> Sort code: [00-00-00] | Account number: [00000000]
> [For EU clients: IBAN: [.......] | BIC: [.......]]
> Reference: [2026-001]
> [Or pay by card here: [Stripe payment link]]
>
> Thank you! Work begins as soon as the deposit arrives, and I'll confirm the same day.

Housekeeping: number invoices sequentially, keep a copy of every one, and chase politely at 7 days ("Just a nudge on invoice 2026-001, let me know if anything's made paying awkward") because with a 50% deposit structure you are never owed money for undelivered work, only delivered.

## Template F: the testimonial request

Send within 48 hours of the client's happy reaction to the delivered work, while the glow is real. Not before the balance is paid.

> Hi [name],
>
> I'm so glad you're happy with the room, honestly, messages like yours are the best part of this job.
>
> Could I ask a small favour? I'm building my portfolio and a couple of sentences from you would make a real difference. Nothing formal, and three prompts in case they help:
>
> - What was the room like before, in a sentence?
> - What's different now (in the room, or in how you use it)?
> - Would you recommend working with me, and to whom?
>
> If you'd rather, just reply with whatever you'd tell a friend who asked "so how was it?", and I'll check with you before using a word of it anywhere.
>
> And may I use the before and after photos alongside it? First name only, or fully anonymous, whichever you prefer.
>
> Thank you again, [name]. It was a genuine pleasure.
> [Name]

When it arrives: save it somewhere permanent immediately, confirm the exact wording and credit they are comfortable with, and put it to work the same week: portfolio page, Instagram, and the bottom of your proposal template, where the next client will read it at the exact moment they are deciding whether to trust you.
