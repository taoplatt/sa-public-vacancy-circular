# Start here

Welcome to The First Client Kit. You bought this because you love interiors and you want someone to pay you for that, at least once, so you know it is real. That is exactly what this kit is built to do. Not a career's worth of theory. One paying client, in about 30 days, for a fee somewhere between £75 and £500 depending on the offer you choose.

Everything in here is scoped to that first client. We are not going to talk about scaling, studios or hiring. We are going to talk about picking one small offer, building three pieces of proof, telling the right 40 people about it, and delivering one room's worth of work well enough that the client says thank you and someone else hears about it.

## What is in the kit

- **Start here** (this file). The permission facts and how to use the kit.
- **The first client roadmap.** A 30-day, week-by-week path from nothing to a paid brief, with every outreach script written out word for word.
- **The pricing guide.** How to set your first fees without guessing, including what to say when someone tells you it is expensive.
- **Client templates.** The enquiry reply, the discovery questionnaire, the one-page proposal, a plain-English letter of agreement, invoice text and the testimonial request. Copy, personalise, send.
- **The tool stack.** The free and cheap tools that cover everything the roadmap asks you to do, and where each one fits.
- **The launch checklist.** The whole 30 days as one printable tick-box page.
- **The pricing calculator** (a spreadsheet). Open it in Excel or Google Sheets, type your hours and your target rate, and it suggests a fixed price for each offer.

## How to use it in 30 days

Read this file and the roadmap today. That is about 40 minutes. Then work the roadmap one week at a time:

- **Week 1: decide.** Pick your lane, define one starter offer, set one fixed price. A few evenings of focused decisions, not research.
- **Week 2: build proof.** Create a three-piece portfolio without waiting for clients. This is the busiest week, roughly 8 to 12 hours.
- **Week 3: tell people.** Run the three warm circles outreach. The scripts are written for you. An hour a day is enough.
- **Week 4: close and deliver.** Take the enquiry to a discovery call, send a quote, get a yes, do the work.

Dip into the pricing guide during week 1, the tool stack during week 2, and the templates from week 3 onwards. The launch checklist ties it all together; print it and stick it somewhere you will see it.

Two honest notes on the timeline. First, 30 days assumes you can find 5 to 8 hours a week. If you can only find three, take 60 days; the order of the steps matters far more than the speed. Second, week 4 depends on another human saying yes, which you cannot fully control. What you can control is whether 40 warm-ish people know about a clear, well-priced offer by day 21. Do that and an enquiry is very likely; the roadmap tells you what to do if it is slower.

## The permission facts

This is the part the industry rarely says out loud, so we will say it plainly.

**In the UK, "interior designer" is not a protected title and interior design is not a regulated profession.** There is no licence, no register and no legally required qualification. You may lawfully charge money for interior design advice, moodboards, colour consultations, room styling, sourcing lists and e-design packages today. The title "architect" is different: it is protected by law (in the UK under the Architects Act 1997) and you must never use it or imply it. But you are not selling architecture. You are selling design and decoration, and that is open to you.

**Across most of Europe the position is the same for the work in this kit.** Interior decoration, styling, colour advice and e-design are, in most countries, unregulated commercial services that anyone may offer. A small number of countries protect the specific title "interior architect" (for example Germany's Innenarchitekt, which requires chamber registration). The simple, safe rule everywhere: describe yourself as an interior designer, interior stylist, home stylist or colour consultant, never as any kind of architect, and spend five minutes checking your own country's rules on titles if you are outside the UK. The activity of advising on furnishings, colour and layout is not the regulated part; the architect title and structural work are.

**What is regulated, and stays firmly outside your offer:** anything structural or architectural. Removing or altering walls, chimney breasts or staircases, changing the building's structure, electrics, gas and plumbing work, extensions, loft conversions and anything needing planning permission or building regulations approval. When a client's wishes drift that way (and they will, usually as a casual "could we just knock this wall through?"), your answer is ready-made: "That is structural, so it needs a structural engineer or architect and possibly building control approval. I can recommend you speak to one, and I will design around whatever they say." Saying this makes you look more professional, not less. The letter of agreement in the templates file includes this disclaimer in writing.

Two sensible grown-up steps that are not legally required but which we recommend before you invoice anyone: tell your tax authority you have self-employed income (in the UK that is registering for Self Assessment with HMRC, which is free and takes minutes online; elsewhere, your national equivalent), and get basic professional indemnity and public liability insurance, which for a sole-trader designer typically costs less than a tank of petrol per year. Neither is a barrier. Both let you sleep.

## What this kit will not do

We would rather you trust the kit than be dazzled by it, so here is the honest edge of it.

- **It will not guarantee you income.** Nobody can, and anyone who promises it is selling you a feeling. This kit gives you the same path that working designers actually used for their first client: a clear offer, visible proof, direct outreach. The path works when it is walked.
- **It will not remove the work.** Week 2 involves genuinely designing three rooms. Week 3 involves sending real messages to real people, which is uncomfortable for roughly everyone. There is no shortcut past either, and the scripts only make it easier, not effortless.
- **It will not make you a qualified architect, and it does not pretend to.** Structural work stays with the regulated professions. Your lane is design and decoration, which happens to be the lane most homeowners actually need help with.
- **It will not replace legal, tax or insurance advice.** The letter of agreement and invoice text are solid starting templates, clearly marked as templates. Have them checked for your country before you rely on them.
- **It will not build you a brand, a following or a full order book.** One client. That is the promise, because that is the moment everything changes: the first time someone who is not your mum pays you for your eye. After that, you are not an aspiring designer. You are a designer with a client, and client two is a much shorter walk.

Turn to the roadmap. Week 1 starts with one decision, and you can make it tonight.
